Parasites Affecting Cattle & Environmental Risk Factors

Parasite prevalence in cattle farms is strongly influenced by environmental conditions. Temperature, humidity, rainfall, pasture management, and stocking density all contribute to parasite survival and transmission risk.

Internal Parasites (Endoparasites)

  • Gastrointestinal Roundworms (Ostertagia, Cooperia)
    Thrive in moderate temperatures (10–25°C) and moist pasture conditions. High rainfall and humidity increase larval survival on grass.
  • Lungworms (Dictyocaulus viviparus)
    Spread rapidly in wet grazing conditions. Risk increases after periods of rainfall followed by mild temperatures.
  • Liver Flukes (Fasciola hepatica)
    Strongly associated with wet, marshy land and high rainfall areas. Depend on freshwater snails as intermediate hosts.
  • Coccidia
    Thrive in warm, humid environments and areas with high stocking density. Often present in poorly drained housing conditions.

External Parasites (Ectoparasites)

  • Ticks
    Prefer warm temperatures (above 7–10°C) and high humidity. Common in wooded, bushy, or tall grass environments.
  • Lice
    More common in colder months when cattle have thicker coats and are housed indoors.
  • Mites (Mange)
    Spread in crowded housing during winter. Dry, poorly ventilated housing increases transmission.
  • Flies (Horn flies, Stable flies)
    Peak during warm summer months. Thrive in manure-rich, humid farm environments.

Key Environmental Risk Factors

  • Temperature: Warmer climates increase parasite development speed and survival.
  • Humidity: High humidity improves egg and larval survival on pasture.
  • Rainfall: Promotes larval migration onto grass and supports intermediate hosts.
  • Soil Type & Drainage: Poor drainage increases fluke and worm survival.
  • Stocking Density: Overgrazing increases pasture contamination.
  • Seasonality: Spring and summer typically show higher parasite burdens.
